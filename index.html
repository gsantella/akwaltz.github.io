Our Star System

by Alanna Walter

It's elementary!
Since the beginning of time, mankind has looked upon the sky and wondered...

It's led to philosophy and religion, suspicions and insights, and good and bad sci-fi movies. It's struck fear into people then and now with omens of destruction and life, harbingers of gods and myth, and foretellings of the future.
One thing seems surely true: But we can barely begin to understand the deeper parts of the cosmos if we don't know enough about our own neighborhood!

Mercury

Diameter: 4878 km (3000 miles)
Distance from Sun: 57,910,000 km (35,983,606 miles)
Orbit: 88 Earth days
Day length: anti-clockwise, 176 Earth days
Number of moons: 0
Temperature: range, -173°C (-280°F) to 427°C (800°F)

  Mercury is considered the least understood of the Terrestrial planets. Its surface is heavily cratered with mare-like plains like on our moon, indicating the absence of geological activity for billions of years. Its surface has mountains, valleys, plains, and troughs, and it has little to no atmosphere with trace amounts of argon, nitrogen, carbon dioxide, water vapor, xenon, krypton, and neon. It's believed to have a molten core that takes up almost half of the planet's volume. Mercury's core has a high iron content, and its surface holds high potassium and sulphur levels. The largest crater on its surface is Caloris Basin with a diameter of 1550 km (963 miles).

Venus

Diameter: 12,092 km (7514 miles)
Distance from Sun: 108,000,000 km (67,108,089 miles)
Orbit: 225 Earth days
Day length: clockwise, 243 Earth days
Number of moons: 0
Temperature: isothermal, 462°C (863°F)

  Though Venus is often called Earth's sister planet, the only thing they have in common is size. Venus is by far the hottest planet in our star system due to a runaway greenhouse effect. Its atmosphere is extremely dense and is comprised mainly of carbon dioxide and a small amount of nitrogen. Above that are thick clouds of sulfer dioxide and sulfuric acid that produce acid rain. Everyday is a bad day to visit Venus! The clouds are also capable of producing lightning. We've never seen the dark surface of Venus because of this atmosphere.
  Studies suggest that Venus' atmosphere was much more like Earth's billions of years ago, and that substantial amounts of liquid water existed. The evaporation of this water though may have caused or helped cause the runaway greenhouse effect that turned the planet into our star system's official hell. There is a possibility though that resilient life exists somewhere in the lower and middle cloud layers where the temperature and conditions are milder!

Mars

Diameter: 3390 km (2106 miles)
Distance from Sun: 227,900,000 km (141,600,000 miles)
Orbit: 687 Earth days
Day length: anti-clockwise, 24.7 Earth hours
Number of moons: 2
Temperature: range, -143°C (-225°F) to 35°C (95°F)

  Known as the "Red Planet" due to finely grained iron oxide dust prevalent on its surface, Mars has been a spotlight figure in science, science-fiction, and entertainment for a long time. Its rocky silicon/oxygen/metal surface is marked with craters, volcanoes, valleys, deserts, and even polar ice caps! Geological evidence suggests Mars long ago had large amounts of water on it, and these ice caps at the north and south are 70% water ice. The rest is frozen CO2 or dry ice. When the poles are exposed to sunlight during certain seasons, the dry ice sublimes and creates powerful winds that sweep off the poles as fast as 400 km/h (250 mph).
  A catastrophic impact may have occurred around four-billion years ago on Mars, resulting in Borealis Basin which spans 10,600 by 8,500 km (6600 by 5300 miles), covering 40% of the planet's surface! Mars is also the site of the largest volcano in the system: Olympus Mons with a height of 25 km (16 miles)- 3-times the height of Mount Everest.
  Seven functioning spacecraft study Mars: five in orbit and two on the surface, MER Opportunity and MSL Curiosity. The start of colonization on Mars is planned to begin within this generation's lifetime.

Jupiter

Diameter: 140,000 km (about 90,000 miles)
Distance from Sun: 778,000,000 million km (483,426,788 miles)
Orbit: 11.86 Earth years
Day length: clockwise, 9.93 Earth hours
Number of moons: 67
Temperature: average -108°C (-162°F)

  Jupiter, king of the Planets: within its turbulent atmosphere, its most prominent feature is the Giant Red Spot. It's an anticyclonic storm that's larger than Earth itself (24–40,000 km by 12–14,000 km) and known to have existed since the 17th century when it was first viewed by telescope. From within this storm, winds howl at up to 425 miles/hour. The storm rotates differentially in respect to the rest of the atmosphere.
  As with gas giants, Jupiter's core composition is unknown. None of our technology can survive the extreme pressure of the atmosphere to even get a glimpse of what's hiding beneath of the miles of clouds. Jupiter has a toxic gas giant-typical atmosphere comprised of hydrogen, helium, methane, and ammonia mostly.
  Jupiter has many moons, and several of them are extremely interesting, many regarding the possibility of extraterrestrial life. They are: Europa (in particular), Io, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Saturn

Diameter: 120,536 km (about 74,898 miles)
Distance from Sun: 1,433,000,000 km (890,700,000 miles)
Orbit: 29.46 Earth years
Day length: clockwise, 10.6 Earth hours
Number of moons: 62 with formal designations
Temperature: average -139°C (-218°F)

  Like the other gas giants, Saturn's atmosphere is mostly hydrogen and helium. Its innards are believed to consist of iron-nickel and rock surrounded by a deep layer of metallic hydrogen and liquid hydrogen and helium. Saturn's pale yellow hue is created by ammonia crystals in its upper atmosphere. It has the second-fastest wind speeds in the solar system: 1119 miles/hour! Nothing on Earth would be able to withstand those wind speeds.
  Saturn's most obvious feature is its rings, which are merely made of ice particles and a smaller amount of rocky dust and debris in many bands that vary in width, color, and density. These planetary rings extend as far as 120,700 km above Saturn's equator. Saturn has seasons and brilliant aurorae at its poles as well!

Uranus

Diameter: 51,120 km (31,764.5 miles)
Distance from Sun: 2,877,000,000 km (1,787,000,000 miles)
Orbit: 84 Earth years
Day length: clockwise, 17.3 Earth hours
Number of moons: 27
Temperature: average -197°C (-323°F)

  Though Uranus' cyan atmosphere is mostly hydrogen and helium like other gas giants, it contains more water, ammonia, and methane ices than other planets. The outer atmosphere of methane has absorption bands in the visible and near-infrared spectrum, giving the planet its aqua color. But an interesting feature is that Uranus' axis of rotation is nearly parallel to that of the other planets; each of its poles get around 42 years of continuous sunlight and then 42 of darkness! That also means that its set of planetary rings are also parallel. The rings are estimated to only be 600-million years old, which is young compared to the rest.
  Some lab experiences suggest that, because of Uranus' composition and extreme pressure and temperature deep within, an ocean of liquid diamond may exist between the mantle, complete with "diamond-bergs".

Neptune

Diameter: 49,250 km (30,603 miles)
Distance from Sun: 4,500,000,000 km (2,796,170,365 miles)
Orbit: 165 Earth years
Day length: clockwise, 16 Earth hours
Number of moons: 14
Temperature: -218°C (-360°F) at cloud-tops; 5100°C (9212°F) at center

  Neptune is the only planet to be discovered by mathematical means due to a pull on its neighbor Uranus' orbit and not by observation. Its atmosphere has visible, active weather patterns. A Great Dark Spot like Jupiter's was observed in 1989 by Voyager 2. Despite being so far from the sun, Neptune radiates 2.5 times the energy that it receives from the sun. Like Uranus, the source of this internal heat is unknown. This is believed to drive the weather and strongest winds in our system: at 1300 miles/hour, they would strip Earth of everything we know in less than a minute.
  Also like Uranus, Neptune my also have diamond oceans deep within.

Pluto

Diameter: 2370 km (1473 miles)
Distance from Sun: 7,500,000,000 km (4,660,283,942 miles)
Orbit: 248 Earth years
Day length: clockwise, 6.4 Earth days
Number of moons: 5
Temperature: average -230°C (-382°F)

  As of 2006, Pluto was not considered a planet anymore; it's a minor or dwarf-planet, like Ceres and Eris. Because it's so far away, not much is known about it other than what we can see; however, the Pluto system is due to be explored in July of 2015 by probe New Horizons.
  Pluto's orbit is inclined and highly eccentric. At certain times, shortly, Pluto is closer to the sun than Neptune; it last happened between 1979 and 1999, and it won't be happening again for another 2 generations. It also rotates on its side like Uranus, causing extreme seasons that shroud large portions of the object in total light and darkness for many years.
  Pluto and its largest satellite Charon (which is a little over half the size of Pluto) are gravitationally locked to each other so the same face always face the other. They revolve around each other every 6.4 Earth days.
